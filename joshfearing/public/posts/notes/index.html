      • 1.2: requirements

        • how to build a computer network from the ground up

        • 1.2.1 stakeholders

          • app programmer: list services his app needs
          • network operator: list characteristics of system that is easy to administer and manage
          • network designer: list properties of cost-effective design

        • 1.2.2 scalable connectivity

          • at lowest level: network is 2 computers connected by physical medium (optical fibre, for instance)
            • 2 nodes, 1 link
          • switched networks
            • circuit switch: telephone system
              • continous, uninterrupted data that reserves a link
              • maximize quality of service guarantee
            • packet switch: most computer networks
              • divide data into blocks, send on indeterminate routes via store and forward in switches (intermediate nodes that implement the network)
              • maximize efficiency
          • internetwork, or internet
            • network of networks
          • nodes are byte-addressable
            • each node gets an address that identifies it
            • routing: process of determining how to forward messages based on address

        • 1.2.3 cost-effective resource sharing

          • how do lots of hosts share the same network? the same link?
          • multi-plexing: a resource is shard among multiple users…e.g. timesharing a physical processsor
            • synchronous time-division multiplexing (STDM): in round-robin fashion, give each data-flow a chance to send data over the link
            • frequency-division multipexing (FDM): transmit each flow over a different frequency
            • statistical multiplexing: each flow sends sequence of packets over physical link, with a decision made on packet-by-packet basis as to which flow’s packet to send next
              • packet is the granularity with which links are allocated to different flows

        • 1.2.4 support for common services

          • a network must do a lot more complicated things than just send a message
          • 2 apps could develop their own complicated functionality; easier for the network to handle it, and the apps to avail themselves of it
          • logical process to process channels are implemented on top of a collection of physical host-to-host channels
          • common communication patterns
            • NFS: client reads file from server
            • request/reply channels, message stream channel
            • strive for the smallest number of abstract channel types that can serve the largest number of applications
          • reliable message delivery
            • 3 kinds of failure
              • bit errors: 0 becomes 1; very rare.
              • packet loss; distinguishing between lost and late packets
              • node and link failures: crashes

        • 1.2.5 manageability

          • upgrading equipment
          • troubleshooting issues
          • adding new features
          • automated management is incredibly important as networks begin to scale enormously
          • avoid change = way to make network easier to manage
            • favoring stability is the approach to the telecommunications industry, so very slow moving and risk averse
            • the cloud has changed this though. we are moving fast again. managing a rapidly evolving network is arguably the central challenge now

    • 3

      • 3.1 switching basics

        • switch transfers packets from input to one or more outputs
        • star topology:
          • large networks can be built out of otherwise limited switches by interconnecting
          • adding new host to network by connecting it to a switch doesn’t reduce performance
        • switch uses the header of a packet to figure out where to send it
        • addresses identify end nodes

        • 3.1.1 datagram / connectionless

          • include everything in a packet a switch needs to route it
          • forwarding table: contains mappings of destinations to ports
          • properties:
            • host can send packet anywhere any time; no connection state needs to be established before data packets are sent
            • host has no way of knowing whether destination hosts are up and running, or whether network is capable of delivering it
            • packets are forwarded independent of one another; they take independent routes even if they are going to the same destination
            • a link failure might not

        • 3.1.2 virtual circuit / connection-oriented

          • 2nd technique for packet switching uses the concept of a virtual circuit
          • a virtual connection is established from source to destination
          • vc table: connection state for a single connection consists of an entry in a VC table
          • 1 entry in vc table contains:
            • Incoming packet VCI: virtual circuit identifier that uniquely identifis the connection at this switch
            • incoming interface
            • outgoing interface
            • outgoing packet VCI: matches the incoming packet VCI at the next switch
          • 1 VCI per connection
            • everytime a new connection is created, we need to assign a new VCI for that connection ON EACH LINK that the connection will traverse
            • A-B-C-D = 3 VCIs
          • Setup of VCI
            • A sends setup message to Switch 1, it contains complete destination address of host B; setup needs to get all the way to B to create the necessary connection state in every switch along the way
              • comparable to getting a datagram to B
              • switches have to know which output to send the setup message…circular logic? we’ll get to this later.
            • every next switch, each vc table gets a new entry for the new connection
              • “when packets arrive on port 2 with identifier 5, send them out on port 1”
            • once the setup messages reaches B, there is now a full complete virtual circuit from A to B
            • when A no longer wants to send messages to B, it sends a teardown message along the switches

          • things to note about virtual circuit switching

            • 1 RTT of delay before data can be sent, for the setup message
            • the connection request contains the full address for host B;
            • each data packet contains only a small identifier, unique on only 1 link
              • per packet overhead is reduced
              • virtual circuit number can be treated as an index into a table rather than a key to be looked up
            • if a switch/link in a connection fails, the connection is broken and needs to be torn down and replaced by a new one
            • how do switches decide which link to forward the connection request? glossing over now. routing algorithm to come.
            • the nice thing about VCs is that once the setup message is sent, the host knows quite a bit about the network;
            • by comparison, datagram network has no connection establishment phase. each switch processes packets independently…each arriving packet competes with others for buffer space. no space? discard the packet
            • made VPNs possible

          • Asynchronous Transfer Mode (ATM)

            • successful example of virtual circuit technologies
            • 80s and 90s popular;

        • source routing

          • uses neither virtual circuits nor datagrams
          • all the info about network topology is required to switch a packet across the network is provided by the source host
          • list of output ports in ordered list, rotating at each switch: 301 130 013 done
          • this approach assumes that host A knows enough about the topology of the network to form a header that has all the right directions
          • cannot predict how big the header needs to be
          • rather than rotate the header, we could strip the first element..but then how do we get it back to A

    • why US sucks at transit

      • we obsess over mode of transit, rather than where we need transit
      • we think about a line in isolation from other networks
      • we focus on single purpose transit
      • we don’t use data. we go by assumptions.
        • When public meetings are held, the people who show up are usually not representative of the population as a whole. Some of the people who need transit most-like low-income families juggling multiple jobs-do not have time to come to an open house. But they do show up in the data.
      • we think at too large a scale
      1. role of transit in america

      • intro

        • transit allows everyone to get around, to have interesting and fulfilling lives, to be full and productive members of society
        • we have a “coverage service” approach to roads in most cities…every road is plowed, every road is paved
        • transit only works if integrated into a walkable, mixed-use place; there’s no functional transit without walkability

      • history of transit in us

        • streetcars were commonplace, EVERYWHERE: Missoula, Biloxi, even Madison, :D
        • lots of stuff

      • modes

        • streetcars:
          • least reliable, least expensive, lowest capacity rail transit
          • overhead wire
        • light rail:
          • overhead wire
          • most common rail transit in us
          • can be integrated with traffic or completely separated
        • BRT:
          • dedicated lanes (most important)
          • high quality stations
          • branding that treats service as distinct from local bus service
          • open: extend beyond dedicated lanes
          • closed: confined
        • heavy rail:
          • highest cap
          • powered by electric third rails
            • electricutes anyone who touches it, so requires full-grade separation in a subway, elevated structure, etc.
            • most expensive to build
            • most are fully automated / driverless > lower operating costs > higher frequency service
        • commuter rail:
          • 9-5 oriented

      • hopes and fears

        • reasons for:
          • transit is the only antidote to congestion
          • helping people who cannot afford a car
            • arguments framed around transit-dependent riders implies people who need transit use it already…in reality, plenty of people are car-poor
          • environmental
        • against:
          • the cost of transit
          • transit > crime
            • no clear evidence for this
            • mostly just fear of poor people
          • government handout stigma
          • attack on “freedom”
          • gentrification?
            • problem of scarcity though; if we only have a few lines of high-quality transit, the places it serves becomes uniquely valuable. if we had it everywhere, this wouldn’t happen
          • racism

      1. good transit

      • density

        • 10,000 people per sq. mile justifies frequent service
        • put transit in dense areas? good. but neighborhoods change.
        • put people by transit?
          • this happens organically;
          • mixed use around transit is the result of market demand, not the plotting of city plans
            • the reverse is usually true
        • in america, we do a bad job at putting transit in dense neighborhoods

          • work

      • walkability

        • transit begins/ends on foot/bike
        • people will walk, at most, a half mile if pedestrian conditions are good. that walkable zone is the key to good transit

      • connectivity

        • transit routes must intersect
        • fare network unity

        • 15 mins is considered frequent; a person can just show up

The Definitive Guide to GTFS

intro

  • gtfs
    • describes public transit system
    • enable PTA’s to upload schedules to google transit
    • a zip file containing CSV files that list routes, stops, trips
  • structure of gtfs feed
    • each route (routes.txt) has one or more trips (trips.txt)
    • each trip visits a series of stops (stops.txt) at specified times (stop_times.txt)

agency.txt

  • represents the agency that provides data for this feed
  • if there are multiple agencies included in this feed, routes.txt makes reference to agencies in this file

stops.txt

  • a record in this file can either be a stop or a station; stations have 1+ child stops, as indicated using parent_station value

other stuff

shapes.txt

  • each trip in trips.txt has a shape associated with it
  • 2+ records in shapes.txt with the same shape_id value defines a shape
    • literally plotting the exact route with coords
  • very large file

DONE make sweet potato pie

Servings: 8 Yield: 1 (9-inch) pie

  • Ingredients

    • 1 (1-pound) sweet potato, with skin
    • 1/2 cup butter, softened
    • 1 cup white sugar
    • 1/2 cup milk
    • 2 large eggs
    • 1/2 teaspoon ground nutmeg
    • 1/2 teaspoon ground cinnamon
    • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
    • 1 (9-inch) unbaked pie crust

  • Directions

    • Gather the ingredients.
    • Place whole sweet potato in pot and cover with water; bring to a boil. Boil until tender when pierced with a fork, 40 to 50 minutes.
    • Preheat the oven to 350 degrees F (175 degrees C).
    • Remove sweet potato from the pot and run under cold water. Remove and discard skin. Break sweet potato flesh apart and place in a bowl.
    • Add butter and mix with an electric mixer until well combined.
    • Add sugar, milk, eggs, nutmeg, cinnamon, and vanilla; beat on medium speed until mixture is smooth.
    • Pour filling into unbaked pie crust.
    • Bake in the preheated oven until a knife inserted in the center comes out clean, 55 to 60 minutes. Cool completely before serving.

  • Cook’s Note

    I use a food processor to blend the sweet potatoes and filling ingredients together for an extra-smooth filling. The pie will be puffed up like a soufflé when you remove it from the oven, but it will deflate as it cools.

Hi all. I’ve always been a little frustrated with the job system in the house.

  • I’ve been the maintenance person, which per policy is A LOT for one person to do, I felt, despite being very important - whether we have potable water, whether we have a functioning refrigerator, whether our outdoor pipes don’t freeze in the winter depends on this job being done well.
  • I’ve had AR, which is a job that takes like 30 minutes per month to do and, honestly, I wasn’t sure why we needed this as a separate job. Making the billing sheets felt like a redundant task, and people are pretty good about paying rent generally. Basically I felt like I wasn’t contributing meaningfully to the house.
  • Now I’m grocery, and while I started out doing it with a lot of passion, it’s a much bigger time sink: 1.5-2 hours per week after a workday and, honestly, the MASSIVE sprawl and grocery cart battle that is Woodmans – the only place where I can expect to find every single particular thing my housemates want to eat – is beginning to wear on me and it is out of the way of my commute. Not to mention the emotional burden of disappointing some folks in the house on a regular basis and hearing about it implicit/sometimes very explicit ways.
  • If I could shop at Aldi or Costco I would be chilling but they don’t have the variety we crave.

Basically the point of my rambling is that not all jobs are created equal:

  • some jobs can go 100% undone for months and virtually no one cares or says anything; some jobs can be done consistently, but done imperfectly, which nonetheless triggers a whole host of complaints and grievances.
  • some jobs take 1+ hours per week to do.
  • some jobs require physical labor, some mental labor.

Disparity in these jobs is unavoidable. But we should also strive for as much equity as is possible.

  • Much of the jobs go undone. So let’s cut the stuff that can go undone, and actually has gone undone without any significant consequence.

I propose doing so by just cutting the jobs that I feel add very little value to the house.

New jobs

    • With 8 cooks per month, this will mean fewer dinners, technically speaking.

      • Since it will be required weekly on a fixed day of the week, there’s no chaos in when it happens, there’s no rush of everyone trying to fit in a cook at the end of the month.
        • Ideally we should agree upon a night that everyone is generally free to have dinner. This should maximize attendance.
        • Since we know when it will happen, we can plan for it not only from an attendance angle but from a groceries angle.
        • The amount of times I’ve cooked for no one b/c everyone was out of the house is very high. It is kinda demoralizing, honestly.

    • Removing cooking as a requirement

      • I think it is unreasonable and unrealistic to expect everyone to cook.
      • This has been a difficulty in membershipping. We may be excluding more good candidates than we would like to be.
      • Unfortunately, I think one consequence of the universal requirement is that some people just don’t cook or, when they do, it isn’t necessarily stuff that people find appetizing / satisfying / fair. I have a pretty depressing memory of one housemate (now gone) who just made pancake batter and stuck it in the fridge for others to cook later as his “cook”. On the other hand, I’ve had some pretty incredible meals in this house from people passionate about food and cooking. I’d much rather direct the energy and talents of people who don’t like cooking toward jobs that make a real impact they are more suited to…I think everyone will benefit from this.
      • Some people are very clearly inspired to cook regularly and do it regularly. Others don’t. To each their own.

Membershipping / Leases

  • Membership process

    Membershipping follows this process:

    1. Prospective member (PM) fills out application
    2. House reviews application and determines whether to invite (PM) for 1st house dinner, scheduled by Memco
    3. 1st house dinner: casual dinner, ends with a house tour. House then decides to invite PM to 2nd dinner
    4. 2nd house dinner: 1 hour formal interview based on a list of pre-determined questions, shared with PM before the meeting.
    5. At soonest house meeting, the house votes on whether to membership the PM. This discussion is not recorded in house notes. It follows this format:
      • Round Robin (RR) of positives
      • RR of negatives
      • RR of further thoughts
      • Vote. Must be unanimous.
    6. Membershipping means: in a pool of accepted applicants. Further votes may be necessary to narrow down this pool and determine who actually gets a room. This is a ranked vote.

  • Leases

    • Avalon only approves year long contracts
    • All contracts are from August 15th to August 14th of the next year
    • Renewal- all house members must convey their intent to re-sign for the next year by April 1st
    • Previous house members may reapply as any other potential member
    • Members needing to move out prior to the end of their lease are expected to be involved in the process of finding another member to take their place

  • Incoming members

    • New members are exempt from house responsibilities for 1 month.
    • New members can expect to be guided through various house things, but it is also as much their responsibility to ask questions and read house documents as it is for others to educate them.

  • Outgoing members

    • Outgoing members should give at least three months notice before moving out.
    • If members have to move out without 3 months notice, it is encouraged that they negotiate a mutually beneficial contribution to the house before moving out to offset the potential financial and work burden an unexpected vacancy can cause for house members. For instance:
      • Helping with the advertisement of the vacant room: flyering, posting on social media, etc.
      • Cooking for and attending membership dinners

House Meetings

  • House meetings occur every 2 weeks (biweekly) on Sundays 5:30PM-7:30PM or earlier

  • Agenda items

    Agendas must be submitted to the Job Coordinator by Saturday noon before the house meeting. See format below:

    • Name (title of agenda item)
    • Sponsor (person submitting item)
    • Estimated Length (time in mins)
    • Type (discussion or decision)
    • Content (content of item)

  • Job coordinator:

    • Gathers agenda items from house members via email by 12pm the preceding Saturday
    • Assembles the agenda in a Google Doc
    • Takes notes during house meeting

  • Facilitator of the meeting:

    • Is a rotating job.
    • Guides flow of the meeting
    • Keeps track of time
    • Manages speaking order and keeps conversation on track
    • Recaps main takeaways from each item

  • Meeting flow:

    1. Mindfulness check-in
    2. Check-ins (rent, jobs, strikes, well-being)
    3. Committee Check-Ins
    4. Agenda approval
    5. Common space check-ins + Kudos + Check-out

  • Quorum

    • Half of the house needs to be present in order for any meeting to occur
    • Half + 1 of the house needs to be present for any voting to occur

  • Attendance / Proxies

    • No attendance without a proxy = 1 strike
    • If unable to attend house meetings, members can submit a proxy. If a proxy is submitted there will be no strike. Proxy should include:
      • Name
      • Reason for absence
      • Check in
      • Committee check in if appropriate
      • Thoughts on discussion items
      • Votes on voting items
      • Common space check-in/kudos
      • Check out

Absences

Members are recommended to report in advance for absences longer than one week. If members have a trip lasting for at least 7 days, food share can be reimbursed for time gone. The policy for this is:

  • Report at least one week in advance to AP the desire for food share reimbursement
  • If one wishes to combine trips’ length and get reimbursed for that entire duration, one must bring that up at a house meeting - it is up to the house to grant approval.
  • The absent member must get their zone and job responsibilities covered by another member to the best of their abilities.
  • Cleaning shifts are excused without strikes, but the absent member is still encouraged to complete cooking and cleaning shifts within that month if they are able.

Events / Guests

  • Events:

    If it’s happening in common spaces of the house, all house members are welcome. No exceptions. They need not be invited, but they are welcome and entitled to enjoy the space.

    • Large events (10+ non-members)

      • For a large event, the details must be approved at a house meeting and sponsored by a member:
        • When?
        • Where?
        • Who will clean up?

    • Small events / hangouts (4+ non-members)

      • The member hosting over 4 people in the house, whether in their room or in common spaces, must give a heads-up in the group chat 2 hours before and get approval.

  • Guests / Crashers

    • Guest: stays in a member’s room

      • Needs no approval from the house, but the sponsoring member needs to give notice.
      • After 7 days: must pay $5 a day for general expenses.
      • Cannot stay more than 27 days (legal limit for claiming residency) or more than 40 days in a 90 day period.

    • Crash: stays somewhere in common spaces

      • The sponsoring member must get house approval and take full responsibility of the crasher.

Pets

  • Pets must be membershipped with house members, and other house pets.
  • Pets are the responsibility of the pet owners.
  • If absent, pet owners are responsible for finding someone to take care of the pet or temporarily rehoming the pet.
